Case Study

Pregnancy Awareness Application

Health & Wellness Mobile UX Awareness App Figma Illustrator
Pregnancy Awareness · Sonali Hassa · 2025
RoleUI/UX Designer (Solo)
PlatformMobile (Android & iOS)
PublishedOctober 2025

Project Overview

The Pregnancy Awareness Application is a compassionate mobile platform designed to support expecting mothers throughout their pregnancy journey. The app addresses a critical gap in accessible, reliable health information for pregnant women — especially in semi-urban and rural areas of India where doctor consultations may be infrequent. The goal was to create an experience that feels warm, trustworthy, and empowering rather than clinical and overwhelming.

Problem Statement

Many first-time mothers in India struggle with unreliable information sources, anxiety about their pregnancy stages, and lack of clarity around nutrition, symptoms, and warning signs. Existing health apps are either too clinical, English-heavy, or not designed for users with limited tech literacy. The design challenge was: How do we build a pregnancy companion that is informative yet deeply human?

Design Process

1. Research & Empathy MappingConducted secondary research on maternal health literacy gaps in India. Created user personas including first-time mothers (ages 22–32), their concerns, digital literacy levels, and emotional states during pregnancy.
2. Information ArchitectureMapped the full pregnancy journey (Week 1–40) into digestible sections — weekly milestones, symptom trackers, nutrition guides, appointment reminders, and emergency red-flag alerts.
3. Visual LanguageChose a warm, pastel palette with soft purples and peaches to evoke calm and safety. Used custom illustrations (Adobe Illustrator) to represent pregnancy stages with cultural sensitivity and body diversity.
4. Wireframing & PrototypingBuilt low-fidelity wireframes in Figma, then evolved them to high-fidelity screens with micro-interactions and animated transitions using JITTER.
5. Key Screens DesignedOnboarding flow, weekly milestone cards, symptom diary, nutrition tracker, appointment scheduler, and emergency contact panel.

Key Design Decisions

🎨 Warm Visual LanguagePastel gradients and custom illustrations replace harsh medical iconography — creating an emotional connection rather than clinical detachment.
📅 Week-by-Week JourneyBreaking 40 weeks into digestible milestones reduces anxiety and helps users understand exactly where they are in their journey.
🌐 Accessibility-FirstLarge tap targets, high-contrast text, and illustrated content support users with low literacy levels and diverse tech comfort zones.
⚠️ Emergency SignalsRed-flag symptom alerts are prominently designed and immediately actionable, prioritizing maternal safety.

Outcome & Learnings

This project deepened my understanding of designing for emotionally sensitive contexts. The biggest learning was balancing information density with emotional tone — too much data causes anxiety, too little feels patronising. I developed a strong system of visual hierarchy, progressive disclosure, and illustration-based communication that I now apply across projects.

Case Study

JobSeva App

Employment Social Impact Mobile UX Figma
💼
JobSeva · Sonali Hassa · 2025
RoleUI/UX Designer (Solo)
ToolsFigma · Adobe XD
PlatformMobile (Android-first)
FocusBlue-collar Workers

Project Overview

JobSeva ("Seva" meaning service/help in Hindi) is a mobile job-matching application designed specifically for India's massive informal and blue-collar workforce — daily wage workers, domestic helpers, construction workers, delivery personnel, and small-trade artisans. The app aims to connect them with local employers through a simple, accessible, and language-inclusive interface.

Problem Statement

Over 400 million informal workers in India lack access to formal job platforms. Existing platforms (LinkedIn, Naukri) assume high literacy, English fluency, and white-collar contexts. The challenge: How might we design a job platform that genuinely works for someone with a Class 8 education, a low-end Android phone, and limited data?

Design Process

1. User ResearchStudied secondary data on informal sector employment patterns in India. Developed personas — Ramu (construction worker, 34, no smartphone experience) and Priya (domestic helper, 28, basic WhatsApp user).
2. Competitive AnalysisAnalysed platforms like Apna, WorkIndia, and OLX Jobs — identifying gaps in regional language support, voice-based input, and offline capability.
3. Information ArchitectureStripped down to the essentials: Profile setup → Browse local jobs → One-tap apply → WhatsApp connect. Every extra step was a potential dropout point.
4. Visual DesignBold, high-contrast interface with icon-heavy navigation. Minimal text reliance. Local context imagery. Prominent CTA buttons with clear affordances.
5. Key ScreensOnboarding with skill-based profile, location-based job feed, job detail cards, application confirmation, and employer profile.

Key Design Decisions

🗣️ Icon-First NavigationReduced text dependency by using universally understood icons and illustrations — enabling non-literate users to navigate effectively.
📍 Hyper-Local FocusJobs filtered by walking/commuting distance first — because informal workers cannot afford long commutes or relocations.
📲 WhatsApp IntegrationOne-tap connection to employers via WhatsApp — the de facto communication tool for this demographic.
⚡ Minimal LoadLightweight design optimised for 2G/3G connections and entry-level Android devices with limited storage.

Case Study

Easy Loan App

FinTech Trust Design Mobile UX Figma
💰
Easy Loan · Sonali Hassa · 2025
RoleUI/UX Designer (Solo)
ToolsFigma · Prototyping
PlatformMobile (iOS & Android)
DomainFinTech / Lending

Project Overview

Easy Loan is a mobile lending app designed to simplify the personal loan application process for first-time borrowers in India. The FinTech space is rife with predatory UX patterns — hidden charges, anxiety-inducing interfaces, and deliberate complexity that benefits lenders. Easy Loan was designed around the opposite philosophy: radical transparency, reduced anxiety, and genuine user empowerment.

Problem Statement

First-time loan applicants in India face multiple pain points: confusing eligibility criteria, hidden fee structures, unclear EMI calculations, and opaque approval processes. The result is anxiety, abandonment, and exploitation by informal money lenders. The challenge: How do we design a loan app that users trust before they've even submitted an application?

Design Process

1. Heuristic ResearchEvaluated 5 existing lending apps against Nielsen's heuristics. Identified key failure points: error prevention, user control, and visibility of system status were consistently poor.
2. Trust ArchitectureDesigned a "trust ladder" — each screen had to earn the user's confidence before asking for sensitive information. Defined what trustworthy microinteractions look like in a financial context.
3. EMI Calculator FirstPut the loan calculator at the very top of the experience — let users understand cost before commitment. Transparent sliders for amount, tenure, and interest.
4. Step-by-Step ApplicationBroke the multi-page application into a clearly-labelled progress journey — users always know how many steps remain and can go back freely.
5. Approval & Disbursement UXDesigned reassuring confirmation states, real-time status updates, and a clear disbursement timeline — converting the most anxious part of the process into a calm, guided experience.

Key Design Decisions

🔐 Transparency FirstAll fees, interest rates, and total repayment shown upfront — no "learn more" hiding critical information.
📊 Live EMI CalculatorInteractive sliders let users explore loan options before application — reduces cognitive load and builds confidence.
🚦 Progress IndicatorsClear, stepped progress bars remove uncertainty — users always know where they are and what's next.
💬 Plain LanguageAll financial jargon replaced with plain, conversational language — no legal-speak in user-facing text.

Outcome & Learnings

Designing for FinTech taught me that trust is not a visual quality — it's an architectural one.
